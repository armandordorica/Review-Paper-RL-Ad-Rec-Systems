Comments to the Author (required):
Dear Authors,
I have received the reviews of two expert reviewers for your manuscript. Both reviewers agree that the topic is timely and important, in particular for industry practitioners. The potential tutorial value of the paper is highlighted as well.

A number of major issues were however identified. One main issue is that the focus of the work is not always entirely clear. Various discussions in the paper do not seem specific to ad policies and the joint optimization of ads and recommendations. A stronger focus on ad-related aspects is needed. Furthermore, a critical discussion and comparison of approaches seems missing. Questions of evaluating the described methods are missing as well. From a presentation perspective, the work has a good tutorial value in certain places, but lacks a deeper technical discussion at other places.

From my own reading, I conclude that the paper has a lot of potential but I agree with the reviewers that the organization and presentation of the work should be improved and focused. The focus of the work should be strengthened, which might also lead to re-framing of the work in the title and introductory sections, highlighting the introductory/tutorial nature of the work, which is targeted at practitioners in industry. Furthermore, as advised by the reviewers, a clearer separation between ad-related aspects and general recommendation techniques is needed. In this context, clear definitions of the problems are needed at the beginning. E.g., is the goal to select and rank ads independently, or to rank ads and recommendations jointly; or both?

Some parts, on the other hand seem repetitive and partially even not too relevant. For example, the entire discussion of embeddings does not seem too specific for RL approaches, and not many RL approaches are discussed in this section. Similarly, policy learning is explained in some length, but the parts that are specific for ad-related aspects are not sufficiently emphasized. And, as mentioned earlier, questions regarding the practical evaluations should be added. Given that the focus is on industry, at least some overview of common evaluation aspects, e.g., typical KPIs or outcomes of real-world studies, could be added.

As a minor aspect, please do not use references in the abstract, and please use bold-face text sparingly. The acknowledgment section looks quite uncommon, thanking the second author for the guidance.

Reviewer(s)' Comments to Author:

Reviewer: 1

Recommendation: Reject

Comments:
The paper addresses a highly relevant and timely topic: balancing advertising and organic content in recommendation systems. This is an area of growing importance in industry, and the paper’s attempt to survey reinforcement learning approaches in this context is appreciated. The method used to identify relevant citations appears thoughtful, and the broad scope reflects the authors' ambition to cover the landscape comprehensively.

However, the paper currently falls short in several important ways, both in focus and depth. While the topic is compelling, much of the content diverges into pedagogical material or surface-level summaries, at the expense of detailed, critical analysis. This limits the survey's value for practitioners and researchers seeking insight into the state of the art.

# Major Comments

1. Lack of Depth in Core Content

The paper frequently mentions important techniques and papers without offering sufficient explanation or critical evaluation. For instance, references to real-world practices—such as action abstraction in practical systems or the deployment strategies used by YouTube and Meta—are intriguing, but remain under-explored. These examples would greatly benefit from further elaboration, especially given their direct relevance to the paper’s central theme.

A more detailed comparison of methods, highlighting their trade-offs, assumptions, and real-world performance, would substantially enhance the utility of the survey.

2. Overemphasis on Pedagogical Material

A significant portion of the paper is devoted to introductory or tangential content. While some context is helpful, explanations such as the evolution of recommenders from RNNs to Transformers feel out of place, as they are not tightly linked to the core topic of RL for ad policy optimization. Similarly, the Frozen Lake example does not meaningfully contribute to understanding the core problems faced in real-world ad and recommendation systems. The paper would be much stronger with focused, domain-relevant illustrations (e.g., simplified bandit-based ad selection examples).

3.   Evaluation is Underdeveloped

 The paper raises the importance of evaluation but does not offer a systematic review of how evaluation is done in practice or in the cited work. Topics such as common metrics, simulator-based testing (e.g., RecSim), or the role of counterfactual estimators are either absent or only briefly mentioned. Quantitative results from existing studies could also be summarized to help readers understand real-world performance and trade-offs.

4.   Terminology and Definitions

Several technical terms are used before they are defined, which hinders clarity. For example, "displacement cost" appears early but is only briefly defined much later in the appendix. Ensuring that all terms are defined when first introduced would improve accessibility.

5.    Reference Issues

Some citations appear to be misaligned with the claims they are intended to support. For example, a reference supporting the claim that ads underperform relative to organic content actually discusses user-generated reviews versus commercial messaging. Careful review of references is needed to ensure they are accurate and appropriate.

6.   Limited Discussion of Joint Optimization

While utility functions are discussed, the survey does not delve deeply into techniques for jointly optimizing recommendations and ads, especially in multi-objective settings. More discussion of approaches to balancing competing goals (e.g., engagement vs. revenue) and how this is handled in practice would strengthen the paper.

7.   Reward Design and Long-Term Metrics

The challenge of optimizing long-term metrics such as retention is acknowledged, but the discussion remains abstract. More concrete exploration of how surrogate metrics can be identified and used in reward shaping would be helpful.

# Suggestions for Improvement

-    Replace generic pedagogical examples with domain-specific illustrations tied directly to ads and recommendations.

-    Expand on the technical and practical implications of cited work, especially industrial deployments.

-    Provide a clearer framework for comparing approaches—e.g., by organizing around challenges like exploration, long-term optimization, evaluation, or system constraints.

-    Introduce terminology carefully and ensure citations align with claims.

-    Include more on practical evaluation strategies, metrics, simulators, and toolkits.

# Conclusion

This survey sets out to cover an important topic, but in its current form, it reads more as a list of loosely connected references than as a cohesive, critical review. With more focused content, deeper analysis, and improved alignment with the domain’s practical challenges, it has the potential to become a valuable resource for both academics and practitioners.
